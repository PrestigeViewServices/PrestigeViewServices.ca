# Financial Management

How to keep the books straight, the cash flowing, the pricing right,
and the tax man happy. This is owner-level — bookkeeping happens
elsewhere (your bookkeeper or accountant), but YOU read the numbers.

Not tax advice. Confirm specifics with your accountant.

---

## The financial principle

> *"Cash flow is the air. Profit is the muscle. Revenue is the
> noise."*

You can grow revenue and run out of cash. You can be profitable on
paper and bankrupt in the bank. The owner watches all three but
worries about CASH first.

---

## Your numbers stack — top to bottom

1. **Cash position** — what's in the bank right now
2. **Cash flow** — money in vs. money out next 30/60/90 days
3. **Profit & Loss (P&L)** — revenue, expenses, profit by month
4. **Balance sheet** — assets, liabilities, equity (snapshot)
5. **Unit economics** — profit per customer / per visit / per route

Most owners only look at #1 (the bank app). The trap is that the bank
balance lies — money owed to suppliers, taxes coming due, payroll
next week. You need at least #1-3 to manage well.

---

## The 5 numbers to look at every week

Friday afternoon, with coffee, 10 minutes:

1. **Bank balance** (today)
2. **Receivables aging** — how much is owed to us, how old?
3. **This week's revenue** vs. last week + this week last year
4. **This week's jobs done** vs. plan
5. **New customers this week** + source

If something looks off, dig in Monday morning. Don't ruin your
weekend.

## Monthly close — what your bookkeeper does

By the 10th of every month, the prior month should be closed:

- Every transaction categorized
- Bank + credit card reconciled
- Invoices sent for every completed job
- Bills entered for every supplier
- Payroll run + recorded
- P&L generated
- Balance sheet generated
- One-page summary emailed to you

If your bookkeeper can't do this monthly, hire a different one.

---

## P&L review — what to look at every month

| Line | What to ask |
|---|---|
| **Revenue** | Up or down vs. last month / same month last year? Why? |
| **By division** | Mix changing? Is one division shrinking unexpectedly? |
| **Cost of services (labour + supplies)** | What % of revenue? Trending? |
| **Gross profit** | Should be 50%+ for property care services |
| **Operating expenses** | Insurance, rent, marketing — any spikes? |
| **Net profit** | Bottom line — target 15-25% in Year 2+ |
| **Owner pay** | Are you actually paying yourself? Don't skip this |

Anything > 10% variance from plan → 30-minute deep dive.

## Cash flow forecast — 13 weeks rolling

Spreadsheet, updated weekly.

Columns: each week for next 13 weeks.
Rows:
- Opening cash
- Cash IN (collected invoices, by customer if material)
- Cash OUT (payroll, suppliers, rent, insurance, owner draw, tax)
- Net change
- Closing cash

Why 13 weeks? Long enough to spot a crunch coming, short enough to
update weekly without it becoming a chore.

When closing cash drops below 2x weekly payroll in any week → red
alert. Action options:
- Accelerate receivables (call overdue customers)
- Defer non-critical purchases
- Pause discretionary marketing spend
- Owner skips a draw cycle

## Pricing — the highest-leverage decision

A 5% price increase, with no volume loss, goes almost entirely to
the bottom line.

### Pricing principles

1. **Annual review minimum** — every January, do you raise prices?
   Default answer is yes (3-7%) unless customers are pushing back
   hard.
2. **Tell customers proactively** — Dec/Jan email: "We're raising
   prices 5% Feb 1 to keep up with rising costs. Lock in the
   current rate by booking a recurring contract before then."
3. **Per-job vs. recurring** — recurring should always be cheaper per
   visit. The price difference is your insurance against churn.
4. **Bundle pricing** — bundling two services gets a 10-15% bundle
   discount that still beats one-off pricing per item.
5. **Premium tier** — for every standard service, offer a premium
   version. ~30% of customers go premium. Examples:
   - Lawn: Standard mow → Premium with edging every visit
   - Window: Standard cleaning → Premium with screen wash
   - Snow: Standard plow → Premium with full salt every visit

### Pricing red flags

- You're always the cheapest quote — too low
- You win >90% of quotes — too low
- You win <30% of quotes — too high OR wrong target customer
- Sweet spot: **50-65% close rate**

## Bookkeeping — what you do vs. outsource

**You do:**
- Send invoices (or Office does)
- Review P&L monthly
- Approve big expenses
- Talk to your accountant quarterly

**Bookkeeper does:**
- Categorize every transaction
- Reconcile bank + credit card monthly
- Run payroll (or oversee the payroll provider)
- File HST/GST
- Prepare monthly close package

**Accountant does:**
- File annual corporate tax return
- Tax planning (when to take dividends vs. salary, etc.)
- Year-end financial statements
- CRA correspondence
- Strategic advice (when to incorporate, when to buy equipment, etc.)

If you're spending more than 4 hours/month on bookkeeping yourself,
hire help. Your hourly value as the owner is much higher than what a
bookkeeper costs.

## Bank accounts — the structure

Minimum 3 accounts:

1. **Operating** — daily revenue in, daily expenses out
2. **Tax savings** — auto-transfer 25% of every deposit (covers
   HST + corporate income tax + payroll source deductions)
3. **Profit / owner** — auto-transfer 10% of every deposit; this is
   your cushion + owner draw fund

Optionally:
4. **Equipment / Capex** — auto-transfer 5% for next big purchase

Setup once, automate the transfers, then ignore. The cushion grows
quietly while you focus on the business.

## HST / GST

- **Registration** required at $30k revenue threshold (you're past
  this — register if not done)
- **Filing cadence** — monthly, quarterly, or annually depending on
  revenue
- **Charge HST** on every invoice (13% in Ontario)
- **Track input tax credits** (HST paid on business purchases —
  reduces what you remit)
- **Bookkeeper handles** the filing

## Corporate vs. sole proprietor

Talk to your accountant about incorporating if you haven't.
Rough triggers:

- Revenue > $100k/year
- Net profit > $50k/year
- You want liability protection (you should)
- You want flexibility in how you pay yourself (salary vs. dividends)

Incorporation isn't free (~$500-1500 setup + annual filing costs)
but the tax + liability benefits usually justify it once you're
past those thresholds.

---

## Insurance — minimum coverage

Talk to a commercial insurance broker. Minimum:

- **General liability** — $2M minimum, $5M better
- **Property** — your equipment, on-site and in-transit
- **Auto** — commercial vehicle coverage for every truck
- **WSIB** — required in Ontario for most employers
- **Cyber liability** — if you store customer data (DB has it; yes)
- **Errors & Omissions** — protects against property damage claims

Annual review with broker. Premiums grow with revenue + crew size —
budget for it.

## Tax-saving habits (not advice — talk to accountant)

- Track every business expense, even small ones (gas station coffee
  for the crew, etc.)
- Keep mileage log if you use personal vehicle for business
- Home office portion deductible if you work from home
- RRSP contributions reduce personal tax
- Equipment purchases > $500 are capital — depreciation rules apply
- Bonuses to owner vs. dividends — accountant will optimize annually

---

## The "owe yourself" rule

Most owner-operators pay themselves last and worst.

Reverse this:

1. Pay yourself a market salary for the work you do
2. Owner draw / dividend on top, paid quarterly from profits
3. If the business can't afford to pay you a market salary, the
   business isn't viable yet — find the leak (pricing, costs, sales)

A business that doesn't pay its owner is a job that pays badly. The
end goal is a business that pays you well and runs without you.

## Annual financial review with accountant (December)

Block 2 hours. Bring:

- Year-end P&L
- Year-end balance sheet
- Year-end cash flow summary
- This year vs. last year by line
- 3 questions you've been sitting on
- Next year's revenue plan

Walk out with:
- Next year tax strategy
- Any incorporation / restructuring decisions
- Equipment purchases to plan around tax
- Dividend / salary plan for next year
- Bookkeeping process improvements

This single meeting can save you 5-figure tax dollars when done well.

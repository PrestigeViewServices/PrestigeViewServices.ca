# Vendors & Partners

The people outside the company who help you run it. Pick well,
nurture the relationships, review annually.

---

## The principle

Your best vendors and partners are extensions of your team. Treat
them like that — pay on time, respect their time, give them context,
and they'll go the extra mile when you need it.

The cheapest vendor is rarely the best vendor. Reliability beats
price for everything that runs your business.

---

## The core 6 — every small business needs these

You shouldn't go a year without an active relationship with each.

### 1. Accountant
- **Why critical:** Tax filings, year-end statements, strategic
  financial advice, CRA correspondence
- **What you give:** Clean books from bookkeeper, prompt info when
  asked
- **What they give:** Tax-optimised structure, peace of mind,
  proactive advice
- **Cost:** $1,500-3,500/year for a small corp annual + ongoing
- **Pick by:** Small business specialty, responsive (24-48hr email
  reply), explains things in plain English
- **Review:** Annual, after year-end filing

### 2. Bookkeeper
- **Why critical:** Monthly close, payroll, HST/GST filing
- **What you give:** All receipts, invoices, bank access (view-only)
- **What they give:** Monthly P&L by the 10th, clean records,
  CRA-ready everything
- **Cost:** $300-800/month depending on transaction volume
- **Pick by:** QuickBooks certified, references from similar
  businesses, fast turnaround
- **Review:** Annual

### 3. Insurance broker (commercial)
- **Why critical:** Liability, property, auto, WSIB coordination,
  certificates on demand
- **What you give:** Updated equipment list, employee count, vehicle
  details annually
- **What they give:** Right coverage at fair price, insurance
  certificates same-day when customers ask
- **Cost:** Broker is free; you pay premiums (~$200-500/month varies
  with size)
- **Pick by:** Commercial focus, local Valley knowledge, available
  by phone
- **Review:** Annual at renewal

### 4. Lawyer (small business)
- **Why critical:** Incorporation paperwork, employment contracts,
  customer agreements, dispute resolution
- **What you give:** Clear questions, advance notice on big decisions
- **What they give:** Documents that protect you, "should I worry
  about this?" guidance
- **Cost:** $300/hr typical, only when needed (~$1k-3k/year for an
  active small business)
- **Pick by:** Small business focus, available on retainer or
  hourly, talks like a human
- **Review:** Annual touch-base

### 5. Banker (commercial)
- **Why critical:** Operating account, line of credit, equipment
  financing, payment processing
- **What you give:** Annual financials, updates on growth
- **What they give:** Credit lines that grow with you, advocacy
  when you need a fast decision
- **Cost:** Account fees, interest on credit
- **Pick by:** Has small business RM (relationship manager), local
  presence, will pick up the phone
- **Review:** Annual visit + relationship maintenance

### 6. Marketing partner (if you outsource)
- **Why critical:** Consistent content, GBP management, paid ads,
  website maintenance
- **What you give:** Brand assets, monthly check-in time, decisions
  on direction
- **What they give:** Output on cadence, monthly reports, growth
  ideas
- **Cost:** $500-2,500/month depending on scope
- **Pick by:** Local biz portfolio, references, doesn't promise
  miracles
- **Review:** Quarterly performance review; annual scope renewal

## Other vendors / partners (situational)

### Tax advisor (different from accountant — sometimes the same)
- For complex tax planning (incorporation, dividends, family trusts)
- Annual consultation, $500-1,500

### Equipment dealer (per division)
- Mower / equipment service + parts (LawnPros)
- Pressure washer service (ClearView)
- Plow service (SnowLand)
- Loyalty matters — trade discounts kick in for repeat customers

### Truck / fleet mechanic
- Reliable shop that prioritises your vehicles
- Annual relationship — they call YOU when something needs attention

### Branded apparel + sign shop
- Branded shirts, caps, jackets
- Truck magnets / wraps
- Yard signs, door hangers
- Reliable turnaround, consistent quality

### IT / phone provider
- Business phone line (with after-hours voicemail)
- Email hosting
- Internet
- Often the same provider

### CRM / scheduling software vendor
- Whatever tool you use (Jobber, Service Autopilot, Housecall Pro,
  etc.)
- Annual subscription
- Customer support access matters more than feature count

## The annual vendor review (December)

30 minutes per vendor, once a year. Score each on:

| Criterion | Score 1-5 |
|---|---|
| Reliability — did they deliver what they promised | __ |
| Responsiveness — fast and thoughtful replies | __ |
| Cost — fair price for the value | __ |
| Partnership — do they treat us like a partner, not a transaction | __ |
| Growth fit — will they scale with us | __ |

Total < 15 → consider replacing. Total 15-20 → keep, share feedback.
Total 21+ → keep, tell them how much you value them.

## How to find new vendors

- Ask similar local businesses (especially other small
  contractor-style operations)
- Local chamber of commerce
- Google reviews + research
- Local Facebook business groups
- Trade-specific associations

**Avoid:**
- Random Google ads (usually national brokers that re-sell to local
  shops with markup)
- Anyone who cold-calls you with a "great deal"
- Anyone who can't give you 3 local references

---

## The partner test (for any new vendor)

Before signing anything:

1. **Trial period** — start with a single project or 90-day commitment
   before locking in a year
2. **Local references** — call 3, ask "would you hire them again?"
3. **Termination terms** — make sure you can leave if it doesn't work
4. **Single point of contact** — get one name, not a help-desk queue

---

## Building goodwill (the long game)

- Pay every invoice within terms (or early). Vendors talk to other
  vendors.
- Refer them to other businesses when they do good work.
- Send a thank-you card or small gift at year-end (case of beer, gift
  card, hand-written note).
- When something goes wrong, give them a chance to make it right
  before going public.
- When you outgrow them, leave gracefully — don't burn bridges.

A small-business owner who treats vendors well builds a network of
allies. When you need a favor at 6pm on a Saturday, those allies
answer.

---

## Vendor red flags (act fast)

- Slow to return calls / emails (> 3 business days for routine items)
- Surprise charges or scope creep
- Quality dropping over time
- Other businesses warning you about them
- Their contact person turns over frequently
- They go silent for weeks at a time

Don't wait. Replace them at first signal — it doesn't get better.

# Hiring — Office, Management, and Next Crew Lead

How to hire for the seats that take you further out of operations.
Crew hiring is in the division training docs; this is about the
people who help you run the business.

---

## When to hire

Hire BEFORE you desperately need them. The "I'm drowning" hire is
the worst hire — you'll pick the first warm body and live with the
choice.

### The triggers

| Role | Trigger to hire |
|---|---|
| Office / Dispatch | Owner spending > 10 hr/week on intake + scheduling |
| Operations Manager | 2+ active crews + Owner spending > 5 hr/week on dispatch/quality |
| Marketing (part-time) | Revenue > $200k and you're not consistently posting |
| Bookkeeper (already should have) | Day 1. Don't DIY this. |
| Second Crew Lead per division | Existing crew lead capped + customers waiting |
| Lead Generation / Sales | When inbound leads are < 50% of capacity |

---

## The hiring pipeline (always warm, never reactive)

Even when you're not hiring, you should be talking to potential
hires.

### Always-on activities (15 min/week)

- Save every promising résumé that comes in unsolicited
- Note anyone you meet who'd be a great fit (LinkedIn, Facebook,
  in person)
- Build a "future hire" list in the notebook with notes

### Posting (when actively hiring)

- Post on the careers page (already live at `/careers`)
- Cross-post on:
  - Indeed
  - Facebook (your page + local Valley groups)
  - Word-of-mouth (current team + customers)
  - Local trade school if applicable
- Re-post weekly until filled
- DO NOT use generic recruiters for these roles — they don't
  understand small-business culture


## The hiring funnel

```
Posting → Applications → Phone screen → Trial day → Reference → Offer → Start
   100        20            8              3            2          1       1
```

Numbers approximate. The point: you'll talk to a lot of people to
find one good hire. That's normal. Don't lower the bar to fill
faster.

---

## Phone screen (15 min, you or Office)

The point: filter out the obvious no's before investing in-person
time.

### Script

> *"Hi {name}, this is {your name} from Prestige View Services —
> got 15 minutes for a quick chat about the {role} role?"*

### 5 questions
1. *"What got you interested in this role?"*
   (Listen for: do they know what we do? Did they read the posting?)
2. *"Tell me about your most recent job — what worked, what didn't?"*
   (Listen for: ownership of outcomes, no blame on others)
3. *"What's a time you had to handle a difficult customer or
   situation?"*
   (Listen for: calm, accountable, solution-oriented)
4. *"What do you need from a job to be happy a year from now?"*
   (Listen for: do their needs match what we offer?)
5. *"Anything you want to know about us?"*
   (Listen for: did they research us? Quality of questions?)

### Decision rule

After every call: would I want to spend a full day with this person?

- Strong yes → invite to trial day
- Maybe → save résumé, keep open
- No → send a polite decline within 48 hours

## Trial day (paid half-day or full-day)

Best predictor of long-term fit. Better than a 5-round interview.

### Office / Dispatch trial
- Half-day shadowing the current process (you or current Office
  person)
- 1 hour: they answer 3 mock customer calls with you scoring
- 1 hour: they draft a quote from a sample request
- 30 min: they describe how they'd organize a typical day

### Operations Manager trial
- Full day in the field with a crew lead
- They see the work, you see how they handle it
- 1 hour with you reviewing the dispatch board + scheduling tool
- Their assessment: "what would you change in the first 30 days?"
- Your assessment: did they ask good questions? Did the crew lead
  like them?

### Crew Lead trial
- Full day on a route
- They run the equipment (with safety supervision)
- They handle one customer interaction (low-stakes)
- End-of-day: would the existing crew want them back?

### Pay for the trial
Pay them for their time, even if you don't hire them. ~$150 for a
half day, $300 for full. Shows respect, attracts better candidates,
keeps them from feeling exploited.

## Reference checks (15 min each, do 2)

Don't skip this. Most hiring disasters were preventable with a
phone call.

### Who to call
- Their most recent manager (the one they LIST — don't go behind
  their back without permission)
- One peer or coworker from the same job

### Questions

1. *"How did you work together?"* (verify the relationship)
2. *"What were they best at?"*
3. *"What was their growth area?"*
4. *"Would you hire them back?"* (the magic question)
5. *"Anything I should know that I haven't asked?"*

### Listen for hesitation. Listen for what they DON'T say.

If someone says "they were really nice" but can't name one thing
they were great at — that's a soft no in code.

## The offer

Verbal offer first (phone), written second (email same day).

### Verbal script

> *"Hey {name}, we'd love to have you join the team. We're
> thinking {role}, starting {date}, at {compensation}. Can you
> talk through the details now or want a day to think?"*

### Written offer email — includes

- Role title
- Start date
- Compensation (hourly or salary + any bonuses)
- Schedule expectations
- Probation period (90 days standard)
- Reporting line
- Benefits / time off
- A simple "reply to accept" line — no formal contract for entry roles
  (for management roles, draft a formal letter with your accountant)

### What to NOT do in the offer

- Don't surprise them with terms different from the verbal offer
- Don't lowball expecting to negotiate up later — say your real number
- Don't slow-roll the response (3-day deadline max)

## Onboarding plan (first 30 days)

### Day 1
- Welcome breakfast or coffee — you, them, 30 minutes
- Tour of the shop / office
- Issue uniform, keys, login credentials
- Read `00-start-here.md`, `01-vision.md`, `02-org-structure.md`
- Sign safety + employment paperwork

### Week 1
- Shadow current role-holder if applicable
- Read all SOPs relevant to their role
- Meet every team member individually (15 min coffee each)
- End-of-week check-in: what's surprising? what's confusing?

### Week 2
- Begin doing real work, supervised
- Daily 15-minute check-in with you
- One "owner & me" coffee (45 min, you + them, anywhere off-site)

### Week 3-4
- Independent work with check-ins
- 30-day review meeting scheduled

### 30-day review
- 45 min, structured conversation
- 3 questions:
  - What's working for you?
  - What's NOT working?
  - What would you change about how we onboarded you?
- Confirm: are we keeping them past the 90-day probation?

## When to let someone go

Hardest part of hiring is firing. Two rules:

### Hire slow, fire fast
If you're sure within 30 days it's not a fit, exit them now. The
longer you wait, the worse for everyone — them, the team, you.

### The "would I re-hire?" test
If you would NOT re-hire this person today knowing what you know,
you have your answer. Most owner-operators sit with this for months
before acting.

### The exit conversation

1. Schedule it for end of day, in person, private
2. Be direct: *"This isn't working out, and today is your last day."*
3. Have the paperwork ready: final cheque, ROE, return of property
4. Be kind. Don't relitigate. Don't justify excessively.
5. Wish them well. Mean it.

### After

- Tell the team that same day (the team always notices)
- Brief, no-blame: *"{Name} is no longer with us. We wish them well.
  Here's how we're covering their work."*
- Don't gossip. Don't badmouth. The team will respect you for it.

### What this protects

A great team can't tolerate a B-player with the owner's blessing.
The cost of NOT firing is your best people quietly losing faith.

## Compensation philosophy

- Pay above average for the Valley. You attract better.
- Tie variable comp to outcomes you actually want (quality bonus for
  crew leads, retention bonus for office, etc.)
- Annual raises in January. Don't wait for them to ask.
- Bonuses ARE compensation — if you can't pay them when earned,
  don't promise them.
- Equity / profit-sharing for management roles (Year 3+) — talk to
  accountant about structure.

## The "next 3 hires" exercise (do quarterly)

Sit down, 30 minutes, and list:

1. **The next 3 roles** you'll hire for in the next 12 months
2. For each: when, why, budget impact, success metric
3. For each: do you already have someone in mind?
4. For each: what could go wrong, and how do you mitigate?

This forces you to think ahead. The reactive hire is always worse
than the planned one.

# Strategic Planning

The annual + quarterly + monthly rhythm that keeps the business
moving toward the 5-year vision instead of drifting.

---

## The framework

| Horizon | Output | When | Who |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5 years | Vision (in `01-vision.md`) | Updated annually | Owner |
| 1 year | Revenue target + 4 quarterly rocks + hiring plan | December annual planning | Owner + Ops Mgr |
| 90 days | 3 quarterly rocks | First Friday of new quarter | Owner + key team |
| 30 days | Monthly priority | Last Friday of month | Owner |
| 7 days | This week's one thing | Friday wrap | Owner |
| 1 day | Today's 3 most-important tasks | Each morning | Owner |

The further out, the less precision matters. The closer in, the more
specific.

---

## What's a "rock"?

A rock is a 90-day goal that, if accomplished, meaningfully advances
the business. Not a task. Not a routine. A rock.

### Good rocks
- "Hire and onboard one Office / Dispatch person by Mar 15"
- "Increase recurring revenue from 35% to 50% of total revenue"
- "Launch GBP + first 50 reviews by Jun 30"
- "Reach $40k MRR in lawn season"

### Bad rocks (too small, too vague, or routine)
- "Send invoices on time" (that's an SOP, not a rock)
- "Be more profitable" (no metric)
- "Do marketing" (way too vague)
- "Mow more lawns" (a routine, not a goal)

### Rule: 3 rocks max per quarter

You can't focus on more than 3 meaningful initiatives at once. If
you set 5, you'll finish 1. If you set 3, you'll finish 3.

## The annual planning week (December)

Block one week off in your slowest month. No work, no calls. Get out
of the office — cabin, coffee shop, anywhere not your normal
environment.

### Day 1 — Look back
- Re-read last year's annual plan
- Score each rock (done / partial / missed)
- Honest: what worked? what didn't?
- What surprised you?
- What did you learn?

### Day 2 — Look at the numbers
- Full year P&L
- Year-over-year trends
- Best month / worst month — why?
- Top 20% of customers — who are they, what do they have in common?
- Worst 20% of customers — fire them? service them differently?
- Cost spikes — what to address?

### Day 3 — Look at the team
- Each person: in the right seat? promoted? exited?
- Hire plan for next year — what roles, when, what budget
- Compensation review — is anyone undervalued? overvalued?
- Culture: anything brewing? anyone burnt out?

### Day 4 — Look at the market
- Competitors: who's growing? who's struggling? what to learn?
- Customer interviews: call 5 long-term customers, ask:
  - Why do you stay with us?
  - What's one thing we could do better?
  - What's one service you wish we offered?
- Trends: what's changing in the Valley? in property care?

### Day 5 — Plan next year
- Update the 5-year vision (small tweaks, big confirmations)
- Set the year's revenue target
- Set the year's 4 quarterly rocks (one per quarter, ideally)
- Set the year's hiring plan
- Set the year's capital expenditure budget
- Set the year's marketing budget

### Day 6 — Communicate
- Write a one-page "next year vision" to share with the team
- Schedule the all-team meeting for early January
- Update `01-vision.md` with any changes

### Day 7 — Rest
- Take the day. You earned it.

## Quarterly planning weekend (every quarter)

Block one Saturday + Sunday at the start of every quarter. Same
principle as annual, but tighter.

### Saturday (4 hours)
- Score last quarter's rocks honestly
- Numbers review: trends since last quarter
- What needs to change this quarter?
- Set 3 rocks for the new quarter

### Sunday (4 hours)
- Break each rock into weekly milestones
- Identify the OWNER for each rock
- Schedule weekly check-ins (Friday wrap covers this)
- Communicate the rocks to the team Monday morning

## SWOT — once a year, in annual planning

A simple SWOT analysis (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities,
Threats) keeps you honest. Do this in your annual planning week.

### Strengths
What you do better than the competition. What's a moat for you.
- Local brand recognition?
- Recurring contract base?
- Insurance + professionalism vs. fly-by-night competition?
- Trained crew?
- Year-round revenue mix?

### Weaknesses
What you struggle with. What competitors do better.
- Capacity (small crew = can't take big jobs)?
- Marketing knowledge / time?
- Cash flow?
- Dependence on the owner?

### Opportunities
What's changing in the market that you can capture.
- New subdivisions being built in the Valley?
- Aging-in-place demographic needing year-round care?
- Commercial property managers tired of multiple vendors?
- AI / automation lowering operational cost?

### Threats
What's changing that could hurt you.
- New competitor entering?
- Rising labor cost?
- Weather pattern changes (less snow = less SnowLand revenue)?
- Regulatory changes (insurance, WSIB, environmental)?

Then: for each opportunity, can you make a rock? For each threat,
what's the contingency?

## The "stop doing" list (most overlooked)

In planning, we always add. We almost never remove.

Once a year, list everything you're doing as a business:
- Every service offered
- Every marketing channel running
- Every recurring meeting
- Every tool you're paying for
- Every customer relationship you maintain
- Every report you produce

For each, ask: **does this still earn its keep?**

If not — kill it. Cancel the tool. Drop the service. Pause the
campaign. Stop the meeting.

Most businesses' biggest growth lever isn't doing more — it's
doing less of what doesn't matter so you have time for what does.

## When to pivot vs. persist

Some rocks deserve persistence — they're long-term bets. Some need
to be killed.

### Persist when:
- The metric is moving, even slowly, in the right direction
- The underlying logic is still valid
- Early data is mixed but qualitative signals are positive

### Pivot when:
- The metric has been flat or down for 60+ days
- The underlying assumption was wrong
- A competitor figured something out we missed
- The team is losing faith in it

There's no shame in killing a rock. There's shame in flogging a
dead one for 12 more weeks because you're attached to it.

## The annual decision letter to yourself

At the end of every annual planning week, write a one-page letter to
your future self. Date it for next December.

Include:
- Where I think the business will be one year from now
- What I'm worried about
- What I'm most excited about
- One thing I'm committing to next year, no exceptions

Seal it. Open next December at the start of annual planning. Reflect
honestly. Write the next one.

After 5 years you have a record of how your thinking has evolved —
the most valuable journal in the business.
